Solution 1.2.1.1.1.

Assumptions: Suppose that

\begin{align} p\in\mathbb{Q} \text{ and } p\neq 0& \text{ and }q\in\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{Q},\tag{1.2.1}\\ \text{ but that }& pq\in\mathbb{Q}.\tag{1.2.2} \end{align}

By the definition of rational number, the two assumptions above give the existence of integers \(j,k,\ell,m\in\mathbb{Z}\) with 1  \(j\neq 0\text{,}\) \(k\neq 0\text{,}\) and \(m\neq 0\text{,}\) for which

\begin{equation*} p = \frac{j}{k} \qquad \text{ and } \qquad pq = \frac{\ell}{m}. \end{equation*}

Eliminating the \(p\) from the above equations we find that

\begin{align*} \frac{j}{k}q &= \frac{\ell}{m} && \text{and solving for }q \text{ we see}\\ q&= \frac{k\ell}{jm}. \end{align*}

Since \(k,\ell\) are integers by assumption, their product \(x = k\ell\) is also an integer.

Also, since \(j,m\) are integers by assumption, their product \(y = jm\) is also an integer. What's more, since \(j\neq 0\) and \(m\neq 0\) we also know that \(y = jm \neq 0\) since the integers have the zero product property.

Thus \(q = \frac{x}{y}\) where \(x,y\in\mathbb{Z}\) and \(y\neq 0\text{.}\) So by definition of rational number, \(q\in\mathbb{Q}\) is rational. This contradicts our assumption (1.2.1)

Conclusion: Since our assumptions necessitate a contradiction, we conclude by way of contradiction that if \(p\) is rational and nonzero and \(q\) is irrational, then \(pq\) is irrational. \(\square\)
