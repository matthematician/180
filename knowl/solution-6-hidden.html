Let \(x\) be a real number satisfying \(x^2=5\text{.}\) Suppose by way of contradiction that \(x\) is rational.

By definition of rational number, there exist integers \(p,q\in\mathbb{Z}\) with \(\mathop{\rm gcd}(p,q)=1\) and \(q\neq 0\) and for which \(x = p/q\text{.}\)

Since \(x^2=5\) we have

\begin{gather} \left(\frac{p}{q}\right)^2 = 5 \quad \text{ or } \quad p^2 = 5q^2.\tag{1.2.14} \end{gather}

By definition of divisibility, since \(q^2\) is an integer, (1.2.14) implies that \(5 \mid p^2\text{.}\) Then by the result of the previous problem, we have \(5 \mid p\text{.}\) We can conclude as a result that \(25 \mid p^2\text{.}\)

However, since we have assumed \(p\) and \(q\) have no nontrivial common factors, this means that \(5 \nmid q\text{.}\) As a result, we have \(5 \mid 5q^2\) but \(25\nmid 5q^2\text{.}\)

Since \(p^2 = 5q^2\text{,}\) though, it cannot be true that \(25 \mid p^2\) while \(25 \nmid 5q^2\text{.}\) Thus by way of contradiction we conclude that our original assumption that \(x\) was rational cannot be sustained. Hence the square root of 5 is irrational.
