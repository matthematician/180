We will prove this “either-or” statement in two cases.

Case 1: If \(n\) is prime. Then we are done. 4 

Case 2: If \(n\) is not prime. We must prove that the second clause is true: that there exists a prime \(p\leq \sqrt{n}\) such that \(p\mid n\text{.}\)

Suppose by way of contradiction that this is not the case: in other words, suppose that every prime factor \(p\) of \(n\) satisfies \(p \ge\sqrt{n}\text{.}\)

Let \(p\) be a prime factor of \(n\text{.}\) Then there exists an integer \(q\) such that \(n = pq\text{.}\) Since \(p \ge \sqrt{n}\) by assumption, we must have \(q \le \sqrt{n}\) since otherwise, \(pq\) would be greater than \(n\) (when in fact it is equal to \(n\)).

But then, since \(q\) is a factor of \(n\text{,}\) either \(q\) is a prime -- in which case, it is a prime factor of \(n\) which is less than or equal to \(\sqrt{n}\) contrary to our assumption -- or, if \(q\) is not prime, it will have a prime factor \(r \leq q \le\sqrt{n}\) and \(r\) would also be a prime factor of \(n\) less than \(\sqrt{n}\) contrary to assumption.

Therefore by way of contradiction, we conclude that it is not possible for every prime factor of \(n\) to be greater than \(\sqrt{n}\text{,}\) and hence there must exist a prime factor \(p \mid n\) with \(p \leq \sqrt{n}\text{.}\)
