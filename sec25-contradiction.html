Section 1.2 Proof by Contradiction

Worksheet 1.2.1 In-Class Practice (Part 1)

Use proof by contradiction (perhaps along with direct proof) to prove each statement below.

1.

Suppose \(p\) is a non-zero rational number, and \(q\) is irrational. Prove that \(pq\) is irrational.

Solution

Assumptions: Suppose that

\begin{align} p\in\mathbb{Q} \text{ and } p\neq 0& \text{ and }q\in\mathbb{R}\setminus\mathbb{Q},\label{a1}\tag{1.2.1}\\ \text{ but that }& pq\in\mathbb{Q}.\label{a2}\tag{1.2.2} \end{align}

By the definition of rational number, the two assumptions above give the existence of integers \(j,k,\ell,m\in\mathbb{Z}\) with 1  \(j\neq 0\text{,}\) \(k\neq 0\text{,}\) and \(m\neq 0\text{,}\) for which

\begin{equation*} p = \frac{j}{k} \qquad \text{ and } \qquad pq = \frac{\ell}{m}. \end{equation*}

Be sure you convince yourself why each of these three “not zero” statements is true!

Eliminating the \(p\) from the above equations we find that

\begin{align*} \frac{j}{k}q &= \frac{\ell}{m} && \text{and solving for }q \text{ we see}\\ q&= \frac{k\ell}{jm}. \end{align*}

Since \(k,\ell\) are integers by assumption, their product \(x = k\ell\) is also an integer.

Also, since \(j,m\) are integers by assumption, their product \(y = jm\) is also an integer. What's more, since \(j\neq 0\) and \(m\neq 0\) we also know that \(y = jm \neq 0\) since the integers have the zero product property.

Thus \(q = \frac{x}{y}\) where \(x,y\in\mathbb{Z}\) and \(y\neq 0\text{.}\) So by definition of rational number, \(q\in\mathbb{Q}\) is rational. This contradicts our assumption (1.2.1)

Conclusion: Since our assumptions necessitate a contradiction, we conclude by way of contradiction that if \(p\) is rational and nonzero and \(q\) is irrational, then \(pq\) is irrational. \(\square\)

2.

Suppose \(a,b,c\in\mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) If \(a^2+b^2=c^2\text{,}\) then \(a\) is even or \(b\) is even.

Solution

Proof. Let \(a,b,c\in\mathbb{Z}\) be arbitrarily chosen.

Assumptions. Suppose that

\begin{align} a^2+b^2&=c^2\label{ws12-assump1}\tag{1.2.3}\\ \text{but that both }a,b& \text{ are odd}.\label{ws12-assump2}\tag{1.2.4} \end{align}

(See note  2 .)

We've used De Morgan's Laws to negate the “or” into an “and”.

By definition of odd, there exist integers \(k,\ell\in\mathbb{Z}\) such that \(a=2k+1\) and \(b=2\ell+1\text{.}\)

By (1.2.3) we have

\begin{align*} c^2 &= a^2+b^2\\ &= (2k+1)^2 + (2\ell+1)^2\\ &=4k^2+4k+4\ell^2+4\ell+2\\ &=2\bigl[ 2(k^2+k+\ell^2+\ell) + 1 \bigr]. \end{align*}

So on one hand, \(c^2\) is a perfect square. On the other, there exists an integer \(n = k^2+k+\ell^2+\ell\) so that \(c^2 = 2\bigl[ 2n + 1 \bigr]\text{.}\) Setting \(m = 2n+1\) we see that \(c^2 = 2m\) where \(m\) is an odd number.

Thus we know three things about \(c^2\text{:}\)

\begin{align} c^2 & \text{ is a perfect square}\label{cas1}\tag{1.2.5}\\ c^2 &= 2m \text{ is an even number...}\label{cas2}\tag{1.2.6}\\ &\text{ ...and }m\text{ is odd. }\label{cas3}\tag{1.2.7} \end{align}

We proceed by cases.

Case 1: If \(c\) is odd. As we have shown previously, if \(c\) is odd then \(c^2\) is also odd. However this is a contradiction of (1.2.5), so this case cannot be sustained.

Case 2: If \(c\) is even. Then there exists an integer \(r\) such that \(c = 2r\text{.}\) But then

\begin{align*} c^2 &= (2r)^2\\ &= 4\, r^2 \end{align*}

and (1.2.6) implies that

\begin{equation*} 2m = 4r^2. \end{equation*}

Hence \(m = 2\, r^2\text{.}\) However, since \(r^2\) is an integer this implies that \(m\) is even, contradicting (1.2.7), so this case cannot be sustained.

Conclusion: Since our assumptions necessitate a contradiction, we conclude by way of contradiction that if \(a^2+b^2=c^2\) then \(a\) is even or \(b\) is even. \(\square\)

3.

Prove that if \(x\in\mathbb{Z}\text{,}\) then \(4\nmid (x^2+2)\text{.}\)

Solution

Assumptions: Suppose that

\begin{align} x&\in \mathbb{Z}\label{w1}\tag{1.2.8}\\ \text{but that } 4 &\mid (x^2+2).\label{w2}\tag{1.2.9} \end{align}

By definition of divisibility by 4, statement (1.2.9) implies the existence of an integer \(n\in\mathbb{Z}\) so that \(x^2 + 2 = 4n\text{.}\)

Because we assumed \(x\) was an integer ((1.2.8)), we can investigate this assertion by cases, depending on the divisibility of \(x\) by 4.

Case 1: If \(x\) is a multiple of 4. Then there exists an integer \(a\in\mathbb{Z}\) so that \(x=4a\text{.}\) But then

\begin{align*} x^2 + 2 &= (4a)^2 + 2\\ &= 16a^2 + 2\\ &=4\bigl(4a^2\bigr) + 2 \end{align*}

and since \(j=4a^2\) is an integer, we have \(x^2 + 2 = 4j + 2\text{.}\) Thus \((x^2+2)\) cannot be a multiple of \(4\text{,}\) since its remainder when divided by 4 is 2.

Case 2: If \(x\) is one more than a multiple of 4. Then there exists an integer \(b\in\mathbb{Z}\) so that \(x=4b+1\text{.}\) But then

\begin{align*} x^2 + 2 &= (4b+1)^2 + 2\\ &= 16b^2 + 8b +1 2\\ &=4\bigl(4b^2+2b\bigr) + 3 \end{align*}

and since \(k=4b^2+2b\) is an integer, we have \(x^2 + 2 = 4k + 3\text{.}\) Thus \((x^2+2)\) cannot be a multiple of \(4\text{,}\) since its remainder when divided by 4 is 3.

Case 3: If \(x\) is two more than a multiple of 4. Then there exists an integer \(c\in\mathbb{Z}\) so that \(x=4c+2\text{.}\) But then

\begin{align*} x^2 + 2 &= (4c+2)^2 + 2\\ &= 16c^2 +16c + 4+ 2\\ &=4\bigl(4c^2+4c+1\bigr) + 2 \end{align*}

and since \(\ell=4c^2+4c+1\) is an integer, we have \(x^2 + 2 = 4\ell + 2\text{.}\) Thus \((x^2+2)\) cannot be a multiple of \(4\text{,}\) since its remainder when divided by 4 is 2.

Case 4: If \(x\) is three more than a multiple of 4. Then there exists an integer \(d\in\mathbb{Z}\) so that \(x=4d+3\text{.}\) But then

\begin{align*} x^2 + 2 &= (4d+3)^2 + 2\\ &= 16d^2 + 24d + 9 + 2\\ &=4\bigl(4d^2+6d+2\bigr) + 3 \end{align*}

and since \(m=4d^2+6d+2\) is an integer, we have \(x^2 + 2 = 4m + 3\text{.}\) Thus \((x^2+2)\) cannot be a multiple of \(4\text{,}\) since its remainder when divided by 4 is 3.

Conclusion: Since our assumptions necessitate a contradiction, we conclude by way of contradiction that if \(x\in\mathbb{Z}\text{,}\) then \(4\nmid (x^2+2)\text{.}\)

4.

Suppose \(a,b\in\mathbb{Z}\) and that \(a\geq 2\text{.}\) Prove that \(a\nmid b\) or \(a\nmid (b+1)\text{.}\)

Hint

This result can be thought of as saying “The same natural number \(a\) cannot be a factor of two successive numbers \(b,b+1\text{,}\) unless \(a\) is equal to one.”

Solution

Assumptions: Suppose that

\begin{align} a,b\in\mathbb{Z} &\text{ and } a\geq 2,\label{x1}\tag{1.2.10}\\ \text{but that }a\mid b&\text{ and } a\mid (b+1).\label{x2}\tag{1.2.11} \end{align}

(See Note  3 .)

Again we have used De Morgan's Laws for the negation of the “or” into an “and”.

Using the defintion of divisibility by \(a\), by assumption (1.2.11) there exist integers \(n,m\in\mathbb{Z}\) such that

\begin{equation*} b = n\,a \quad \text{ and also } \quad b+1 = m\, a. \end{equation*}

Eliminating the \(b\) from the above equations then shows that

\begin{align} na + 1 &= ma\tag{1.2.12}\\ 1 &= (m-n)a.\label{x3}\tag{1.2.13} \end{align}

However, since \(m,n\) are integers by assumption, we know that \((m-n)\) is an integer. Furthermore, we have assumed that \(a\) is an integer ((1.2.10)).

The only integer solutions to \(xy = 1\) are \(x=y=1\) or \(x=y=-1\text{.}\) Thus (1.2.13) implies that either \(a=1\) or \(a=-1\text{.}\) Neither of these is compatible with the assumption (1.2.10) since we have assumed \(a \geq 2\text{.}\)

Conclusion: Since our assumptions necessitate a contradiction, we conclude by way of contradiction that if \(a,b\in\mathbb{Z}\) and \(a\geq 2\text{,}\) then \(a\nmid b\) or \(a\nmid (b+1)\text{.}\)
